The Creek

Now just a small cove on the East River, Bushwick Inlet was historically the mouth of Bushwick Creek, an extensive inland waterway that included mudflats, salt marshes and tidal creeks covering almost half of the Northside and large parts of southern Greenpoint, including all of what is now McCarren Park. The south branch of Bushwick Creek meandered along Union Avenue and was navigable by rowboat as far south as Metropolitan Avenue. This branch of the creek was fed by three or more fresh water ponds south of Metropolitan Avenue at Havemeyer Street, Marcy Avenue and Rodney Street/Union Avenue.

Two main trunks of the south branch of Bushwick Creek extended due east to about Leonard and Frost Streets, with smaller branches extending to Nassau Avenue and Lorimer Street, and Driggs Avenue and Leonard Street.

Between each of these branches of Bushwick Creek were large areas of salt marshes and wetlands, covering all of today’s McCarren Park and the Greenpoint-Williamsburg Industrial Business Zone (IBZ) to the west of McCarren, and extending inland as far as Humboldt Street to the east and Hope Street to the south. A large island, running roughly from North 13th Street to North 15th Street, and from Wythe Avenue to the eastern end of the McCarren track, separated the different branches of the Creek. Depending on the tides, large parts of the Creek system were variously exposed or covered with salt water. Together with nearby Newtown Creek, the waterway was part of the rich and fertile wetlands that had supported the indigenous populations in the area for centuries.
