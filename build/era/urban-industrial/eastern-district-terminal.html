Eastern District Terminal

With industry dominating the Williamsburg waterfront, manufacturers needed a way to move raw goods and products in and out of the neighborhood. With the advent of the railroad the East River became a barrier rather than a conduit. National freight lines terminated on the Hudson shore of New Jersey and the only way across was to put freight cars onto barges and float them across the harbor. In the 1870s a rail freight terminal was established at Palmer's Dock on the Northside waterfront to handle all these freight cars, expanding to become the Brooklyn Eastern District Terminal (BEDT) in 1906 (organized by the Havemeyers of Domino Sugar fame). A network of rails ran up and down Kent and Wythe avenues and many of the side streets, serving sugar refineries, breweries, barrel makers, and meat packers among many others. BEDT, operational until the 1980s, occupied the waterfront from North 6th to North 11th Street.

The south portion of Brooklyn Eastern District Terminal is now Martha P. Johnson East River State Park. To the north, between North 8th and North 9th Street, lies the first part of Bushwick Inlet Park to open to the public â€“ a soccer field constructed in 2010 and community house built in 2013.

North of that and also once part of the rail terminal is the massive blue and white CitiStorage building. It was the last piece of Bushwick Inlet Park to be acquired, following a long battle by community members to push New York City to live up to its promise to Greenpoint and Williamsburg. After over 10 years of holdouts by the owner and inaction by the city, and in the face of relentless advocacy by the community, the City finally acquired the property in late 2016. Today, the building is vacant and scheduled for demolition by the end of 2024. Prior to leaving office in 2022 Mayor Bill de Blasio allocated $75 million in capital funding to build out the CitiStorage parcels. But first the property requires remediation.
