Williamsburg Bridge

The biggest change to North Brooklyn, though, was the opening of the Williamsburg Bridge in 1903. The second bridge to span the East River, the opening of the bridge transformed Williamsburg, especially the Southside, into a densely developed working-class neighborhood. Larger six-story tenement buildings began to appear throughout the Southside, largely populated by Eastern European Jewish residents who had moved “across the bridge” from the Lower East Side. Non-Jewish immigrants from the Baltic countries, Russia and Ukraine also moved to the area to take advantage of the newer and better housing that was available because of the building boom of the 1900s to 1920s. By 1920, the Southside was one of the most densely populated neighborhoods in the country. 1920 also represented the peak of Williamsburg’s and Greenpoint’s population - combined the two neighborhoods were home to 325,000 people.

Williamsburg and Greenpoint became increasingly integrated into greater New York through the construction of the L train (1914) and the G train (1925), as well as by the construction of the first Kosciusko Bridge in 1937.
