The Founding of Williamsburgh

At the turn of the 19th century, the first attempts to establish a village at the Williamsburg shore were undertaken. Two competing villages – Williamsburgh and Yorkton – were laid out with streets in 1800 to 1802 respectively, the former to the north of Grand Street, the latter to the south. Of the two, Yorkton was marginally more successful, but Williamsburgh won the naming rights (the Williams of Williamsburgh being the name of the surveyor hired by Richard Woodhull, who bought a 13-acre farm that ran from Metropolitan Avenue to Bushwick Creek in 1800).

Williamsburgh Grows

The original vision for Williamsburgh was a suburb of New York City, along the lines of Hezekiah Pierrepont’s Brooklyn Heights. Despite these grand plans, Williamsburgh was slow to develop. It was not until 1827 that the Village of Williamsburgh was incorporated. By that time, it had a population of about 1,000; the rest of Bushwick (including Greenpoint) had just under 600 residents. But the Village of Williamsburgh grew quickly, becoming a town of 5,000 people in 1840 and a city of over 40,000 by 1852. When Williamsburgh was absorbed into the City of Brooklyn in 1855, it had a population of 50,000.

As the village of Williamsburgh grew, it quickly changed from a suburb and resort (an 1827 map of the village shows a “pleasure railway” on the waterfront between North 6th and North 7th Streets, near the willow grove just to the north. A major impetus for the development of Williamsburgh and Greenpoint as industrial centers was the opening if the Erie Canal in 1825. The canal opened up the center of the country to the eastern markets, shifting agricultural production away from places like Bushwick, and creating the need for more shipping along with transshipping from barges to larger vessels.[WD1]
